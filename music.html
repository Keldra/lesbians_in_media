Lesbians in Music

These days, nearly everyone knows Chappell Roan, but she is not the first openly lesbian singer. The earliest might possibly be Claire Waldoff, who was active in Germany from 1903-1942. As we continue on to the 1910's and 1920's, several blues singers were sapphic, including lesbian singer Alberta Hunter who had a lavender marriage (which is likely the case for many queer artists and people of the time) with a man for four years, and mostly kept her sexuality private. In fact, all of the "big three of the blues" (Gertrude "Ma" Rainey, Lucille Bogan, and Bessie Smith) were all sapphic and sang about their experiences with women. It must also be noted that, due to the laws and the way society was structured at the time, it's difficult to know which of these women were lesbians vs which were bisexual.

Calypso Rose, considered to be the mother of calypso music is a lesbian and has recieved every award available to living artsits in the Caribbean. She began singing in 1950 at 15 years old. Though the genre is older than her, she earned her title of being the mother of calypso by being the first woman to win the Trinidad Road March competition. Her songs often addressed issues that she faced, such as racism and sexism.

In the 2010's, we had SOPHIE, a sapphic artist, helping pioneer the hyperpop microgenre. Hyperpop is heavily influenced and created by LGBTQ+ artists and queer culture, especially Ball culture. Other queer women involved in hyperpop include Alice Longyu Gao, Laura Les from 100 Gecs, Slayyyter, and Bell.

All of this history has paved the way for the sapphic and lesbian artists of today. Each person who breaks through in the industry makes it a little easier for the next person. Today we have more openly lesbian artists than ever before, and hopefully this trend can continue so that lesbians will be more accepted in society.
