Lesbians in TV and Movies

Many people know of Arcane, the Netflix TV show based off League of Legends where two of the main characters, Vi and Caitlyn, are both lesbians. Arcane is just one in a history of lesbians being on the screen. The first kiss between two women was actually in the 1922 movie Manslaughter, and it was actually the first kiss between two people of the same gender on the screen, regardless of gender. It would be 8 more years before a leading lady would kiss someone of the same gender, and it would be sapphic actress Marlene Dietrich in Morocco.

Vampires have a very clear translation into lesbian media, as they have always represented the sexual "other", which is something that lesbians can relate to very well in a society that centers men. The first film to depict a lesbian vampire was Dracula's Daughter in 1936. It is the first time that we see a vampire who considers themself to be "afflicted" and needs to be "cured" of their vampirism, which showcases how people of the time viewed queerness. Due to industry standards at the time (Hay's Code), the main character, Countess Marya Zaleska, had to die at the end of the film because of her sexual deviancy. However, starting in the late 1960's continuing into the 1980's, there is an uprise in not just female vampires, but lesbian vampires, a notable example being Daughters of Darkness, which showcases how the civil rights movements happening at the time affected a lot of films, especially horror as horror tends to touch more on the politics of oppression than a lot of other genres. Of course, this built up to Willow and Tara kissing on Buffy the Vampire Slayer (even if they themselves were not vampires). And, of course, the short-run First Kill from 2022, which has a lesbian vampire main character, but was canceled after just one season, which is often the case with tv shows that have lesbian main characters.

While both characters are canonically bisexual, the relationship between Korra and Asami in The Legend of Korra should absolutely be mentioned, as it helped more openly queer female and nonbinary characters come into children's cartoons. Steven Universe had a lot of diversity in characters, including a main character, Garnet, who is a fusion of two lesbian gems, Ruby and Sapphire and showcases a beautiful butchfemme relationship. In addition to Garnet, another main character in Steven Universe, Pearl is also a lesbian. While there were many queer characers in She-Ra and the Princesses of Power, the main character Adora (She-Ra) and her best friend turned enemy turned girlfriend Catra are both lesbians. Disney's The Owl House also has multiple LGBTQ+ characters, including lesbian main character Amity Blight. This only some of the bigger cartoons with lesbian characters, but there are many more. It is especially important for queer characters to be shown, age appropriately, in children's media so that if they are like that, or they have friends or family who may be queer, they will see that it's okay and there's nothing wrong with it, it's just how some people are.

Similarly to yuri manga, yuri anime tends to be far less common than heterosexual anime and tends to only get one season, while other animes may get multiple seasons.
