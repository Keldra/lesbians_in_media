Lesbians in Literature

You cannot talk about lesbians in literature without mentioning Stone Butch Blues by Leslie Feinburg. It is a fictional recounting of a working class butch lesbian in the second half of the 20th century. While it is fictional, Feinburg drew heavily from her own experiences as a butch lesbian and many butches to this day find it relatable in some ways. It is also a book that sheds light on the underrepresented butchfemme community.

Another work that must be mentioned is The Color Purple, which has won the Pulitzer Prize (making Alice Walker the first Black woman to receive the award) and is included on AP English reading lists. The novel has been adapted into many forms, including two movies, a musical, and a radio broadcast. However, it has faced many controversies as well, due to the content, and has spent many years in the list of top 100 banned books.

An early work of fiction that mentions lesbians is Symposium by Plato (300's BC), which is about how pairs of soulmates were all originally one body with two faces. But, even before he was born, Sappho was writing poems. While she wrote about many things, her most famous poems, both at the time and to this day, were her love poems, most of which were written to or about other women. It is because of this that lesbians are called lesbians (as she was born and lived on the island of Lesbos) and that women who love other women, regardless of sexuality, are called sapphic. She was generally highly regarded, and many people, including Plato, referred to her as the "tenth Muse" and she continues to influence writers even now.

Possibly the first novel with a lesbian main character would be Carmilla by Sheridan Le Fanu, an Irish writer. This novella popularized the lesbian vampire trope and is considered to be one of the most influential vampire works, as well as predating Dracula by 25 years. Vampirism has long been a symbol of sexuality (heterosexual, yes, but especially queer). Though some lesbians of today embrace Carmilla, it was originally written to demonize lesbian behavior and uphold heteronormality.

What started as a "a little lesbian joke in an alternative feminist newspaper", the Bechdel test, was started by lesbian cartoonist Alison Bechdel. It has grown from that little joke into being something that is widely known and has had spin-offs for other intersections of lack of privilege. Her most notable works are Dykes to Watch Out For (one of the earliest ongoing representations of lesbians in popular culture, and ran from 1983-2008), Fun Home (a graphic memoir published in 2006), and Are You My Mother? (a second graphic memoir published in 2012).

Luckily, in the modern day, there are plenty of books (fictional and nonfictional) that have lesbians in them, including positive depictions. Unfortunately, they do tend to be less in number and promoted less than their heterosexual counterparts or even compared to stories which depict men together with each other. This is especially shown in Yuri manga. Many end up failing before they can even get official English translations or even a second Japanese volume. Most recently, Love Bullet was on the verge of cancellation, but through a campaign mostly on Twitter, the series was saved and the first volume in Japanese sold out multiple times at multiple stores and had to be restocked. However, not all series get a happy ending. Gal Maid and Villainess: Only Milady's Happy End Will Win is one that was recently cancelled.
